Making sure we grade what you wrote

By Xavier, founder of ParrotTalk · August 7, 2026 · 3 min read

For IELTS Writing feedback to mean anything at all, one thing has to be true without exception: the essay you see on your screen must be the exact essay the AI reads and marks. Early on, in a specific situation, that was not guaranteed, and this is the honest story of the bug I found and fixed.

The trouble lived in the handoff. Between the words on your screen and the words handed to the model for assessment, there was a seam, and in some cases the version that went off to be marked was not quite the version in front of you. The band you got back was real, but it could be answering a slightly different essay than the one you thought you had submitted. That is not a small thing. It is the one promise a marking tool cannot afford to break.

Rebuilding the handoff

I found it the way you find these things: a result came back that did not match the writing, and instead of shrugging it off I chased the mismatch until it confessed. Then I rebuilt how a piece of writing travels from your screen to the assessment, so there is now a single, clear path and no room for a stale copy to sneak in. What you see is what gets marked, full stop.

I am telling you about it plainly because I would want to know, if I were the one trusting a tool with my practice. It is tempting to fix a bug like this quietly and never mention it. But a marking tool that is not open about its own reliability is asking for a trust it has not earned.

Why transparency is the feature

Every tool has bugs. The difference is whether the maker treats correctness as a feature worth chasing or a detail worth hiding. On something people lean on to prepare for an exam that matters to their future, integrity of assessment is not a nice-to-have. It is the product.

The short version: a bug once let the graded essay differ from the one on screen. It is fixed, the path is rebuilt, and what you see is now exactly what gets marked. Free, no sign-up.
