Putting the exam clock on Listening

By Xavier, founder of ParrotTalk · August 3, 2026 · 3 min read

Wondering what computer-delivered IELTS Listening actually feels like under time pressure? On ParrotTalk, Listening now runs on one clock that submits your answers automatically the moment time is up, exactly like the real screen-based test. This is the day I stopped letting people take a timed exam at their leisure.

Before this, my Listening was honest about the questions but dishonest about the pressure. You could pause, wander off, come back an hour later and finish. That is a fine way to learn vocabulary and a terrible way to prepare for an exam whose entire difficulty is that it does not wait for you.

One clock, and a hard stop

So I added a single running timer across the whole section, visible the whole time, with an automatic hand-in when it reaches zero. No frantic scramble, no dialog asking if you are sure. Time is up, your answers are taken as they are, and you see where you stand. That is the real thing, and the small jolt of the clock hitting zero the first time is a useful thing to feel in practice rather than on exam day.

I paired it with two quieter fixes that matter more than they sound. A short glossary for the handful of words that are about the test format rather than the test content, so a candidate is never tripped by a term that was never the point. And clear length guidance on written answers, because "no more than three words" is a rule people lose marks to constantly, and a practice test should teach that rule, not hide it.

Practice should cost you the same things the exam does

The principle underneath all of this is simple. Practice is only worth something if it charges you the same currency the exam does: attention, timing, and the discipline to move on. A test you can pause is a test that has quietly removed the one skill you most need to build.

You can feel the difference on the Listening practice now, and if you want the timing across all four skills in one go, the free mock test puts you on the clock from start to finish. Both are 100% free.

The short version: ParrotTalk's Listening now runs on one exam clock and submits automatically at zero, so it feels like the real computer-delivered test. Free, no sign-up.
